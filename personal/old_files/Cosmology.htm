1  Introduction


1.1   Distances

Distances in astronomy are measured in
Astronomical Units (abbreviated AU)
and in parsec (abbreviated pc):

  • 1 AU is the average distance Earth-Sun: 1 AU = 1.5 ×1011 m

  • 1 parsec is the distance at which a linear size of 1AU corresponds to 1 arcsec on the sky: 1 pc = 3.09 ×1016 m


    The nearest star is roughly 1 pc away.
    The centre of our Galaxy is at a distance of 8.5  kpc.
    The nearest major galaxy (Andromeda, also called M31) is 800  kpc away.
    The nearest larger cluster (Virgo) is 15  Mpc away.
    The nearest supercluster (the 'Great Wall') is roughly at 100 Mpc.
    Quasars are 1 Gpc away.
    The most distant known galaxies are at 3 Gpc.
    The horizon of the Universe is 4 Gpc away.

Cosmology is the study of the Universe at the largest scales, and as such is not interested in anything smaller than clusters of galaxies.

1.3  Assumptions and observations

Cosmology is based on two basic assumptions:

Cosmological Principle: The Universe is homogeneous and isotropic in three-dimensional space,
has always been so and will always remain so.

       homogeneous: appearance is independent of position;
       isotropic: appearance is independent of direction


Galilean invariance: The laws of physics appear the same for observers everywhere in the Universe
that move with uniform velocity.

Olbers paradox: If the Universe were infinitely large, each line of sight would intercept a star, and the night sky would be as bright as the day sky.

3  Laws of Gravitation

3.1   Critical density and Omega

While the Universe is expanding, gravitational forces attempt to slow down and if possible even reverse the expansion. Although gravitation is the weakest of the forces in physics, it dominates at very large distances and it has a long time to act (namely the life time of the Universe). Whether it is strong enough to halt the expansion of the Universe depends on how much mass there is in the Universe.

Assume that the matter in the Universe is uniformly distributed, with density r. Take a galaxy at a distance r from us. The total mass within distance r is the volume of the sphere with radius r times the density r: M=(4/3)pr3 r. The mass of the galaxy is mG.

The total gravitational force on this galaxy can be calculated using Newton's two theorems:


    1. Assume a spherically symmetric distribution of density (e.g. a planet). The total force acting on a test particle at radius r from the origin of the distribution, from the mass located inside this radius can be calculated by assuming that all this mass is located at the origin.
    2. Again assume a spherically symmetric distribution of density, with a test particle located at radius r inside this distribution (e.g. a coal miner). The force on the particle from the mass located outside its radius is zero.

The second theorem can be proven using geomretrical arguments. Proven the first theorem is rather more difficult.

3.3   The deceleration factor q0

At larger distances where we no longer can assume z << 1, we expect deviations from the linear Hubble Law for two reasons:

  • At large distances we see the galaxies at a time when the Universe was younger: the look-back time. This means that we see them at a time they were closer to us than they are at the present time.

  • Gravity is slowing down the expansion of the Universe. In the past a galaxy would have been moving faster. We should see this effect due to the look-back time: distant galaxies should be moving faster than expected from the present-day Hubble Law.

Note that both effects have the same consequence: distant galaxies will be closer to us and moving faster than predicted by the linear Hubble Law.
