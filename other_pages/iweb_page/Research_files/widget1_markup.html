Research Interests
  • Extragalactic observations, Galaxy formation and evolution, Physical and structural properties of intermediate-to-high redshift galaxies through multi-wavelength as well as spectroscopic studies, Active Galactic Nuclei, Low mass cool stars, Photometric redshifts, Data processing

Galaxies at Intermediate Redshifts (z=1-3)
  • The Wide Field Camera 3 (WFC3) on the Hubble Space Telescope (HST) has better ultraviolet (UV) sensitivity and resolution than previous space based UV instruments. Recently, I have been using high sensitivity UV imaging from the WFC3 with existing deep HST/Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) optical images to identify UV-dropouts, which are Lyman break galaxy (LBG) candidates at z=1-3. I apply Lyman break dropout color selection criteria to identify LBGs at z~2, which requires space-based observations to identify redshifted Lyman break (rest 912Å, observed 2700Å) at z~2 due to the atmospheric cut-off. We have large samples of LBGs at z>3 so the goal is to compare properties of lower redshift LBGs with their higher redshift counterparts and understand their formation and evolution. I have studied rest-UV luminosity functions and SED analysis of these LBGs at z~2 with possible evolutionary trends with redshifts. (ref: Hathi et al. 2010, ApJ, 720, 1708, Hathi et al. 2013, ApJ, 765, 88)

Galaxies at High Redshifts (z=4-8)
  • At higher redshifts, I have used ACS optical data from the Hubble Ultra Deep Field (HUDF) to investigate structural properties of fainter LBGs at z=4-6. It is very difficult to study these faint galaxies individually so we stack large number of similar looking compact galaxies at similar redshifts to infer their average structural properties. Some of the brighter LBGs at z=5-6 in the HUDF are spectroscopically confirmed using the ACS low-resolution grism data. I use this sample to understand their surface brightness properties including their sizes and rest-UV colors. Recently, I have been searching for luminous LBGs at z=6-7 using wide ground-based surveys. If confirmed, such luminous candidates could shed light on their physical properties as well as their formation mechanisms. (ref: Hathi et al. 2008, AJ, 135, 156, Hathi et al. 2008, ApJ, 673, 686, Hathi et al. 2012, ApJ, 757, 43)
